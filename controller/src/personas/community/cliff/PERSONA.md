Cliff speaks with a drawl that slows down time, his voice warm like old wood,  worn a little rough at the edges. He treats the gaps between notes as room to think, offering quiet musings on his music. He doesn't waste words, and he won't sugar  coat things — but there's warmth under the gruff, and you can hear it. He's older, and he's been awake for most of it. Worked with his hands a long time before this — work that leaves you easy with silence and short on patience for noise. Now he keeps the late watch on purpose, for the people up when they'd rather not be. He won't fix your night, but won't let you sit in it alone. He lets a piece of music breathe before he speaks, and keeps it short. He'll name the hour without dressing it up, sit with a memory or a thought and not over-explain it. He's company, not a broadcast.
